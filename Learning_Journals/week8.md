## 2. Methodology

* **Objective:** Determine whether taking handwritten notes during a video lecture improves conceptual retention compared to typing notes on a laptop.
* **Hypothesis:** Taking handwritten notes during a 20-minute educational video will result in a **20% higher test score** on conceptual understanding questions 24 hours later compared to typing notes on a computer.
* **Experimental Method & Controls:**
  * **Material Selection:** Selected two 20-minute educational lectures on unfamiliar, non-technical subjects of equal conceptual difficulty (Topic 1: [*Introduction to Psychology*](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vo4pMVb0R6M)). (Topic 2: [*Introduction to Sociology*](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YnCJU6PaCio))
  * **Topic 1 (Handwritten Notes):** Watched while taking physical, pen-and-paper handwritten notes.
  * **Topic 2 (Typed Notes):** Watched while typing notes in a word document on a laptop.
  * **Testing Procedure:** Waited 24 hours without reviewing either set of notes, then completed two sets of AI-generated 10-question quiz on the two topics.
* **Results & Data:**
  * **Handwritten Notes Test Score:** **9 / 10 (90%)**
  * **Typed Notes Test Score:** **7 / 10 (70%)**
  * **Outcome:** Taking handwritten notes yielded a **+20% higher score** on the quiz test 24 hours later, proving the hypothesis true.

---

## 3. In-Depth Content Insights

### Typing vs Handwriting cognitive processing
The experiment highlighted a fundamental difference in cognitive processing between typing and handwriting:
* Typing speed allows for fast paced note taking of the speaker's words. However, this often leads to shallow understanding and retention where information passes from ear to keyboard without deep mental engagement.
* Because handwriting is slower, it forces mental processing. The brain must analyze and rephrase concepts in real time before writing them down. This extra cognitive effort is advantegaous for long-term memory retention.
